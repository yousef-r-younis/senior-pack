The Biggest Mistake Students Make

‚ùå Common Pitfalls

üî¥ Too Ambitious

"I'm going to build a full social media platform with AI recommendations, blockchain security, and AR features!"

Reality: You have one semester, limited resources, and other classes to worry about.

üî¥ Too Boring

"I'll make a simple calculator app..."

Reality: Your evaluators have seen this 100 times. It won't showcase your skills or impress anyone.

üî¥ No Clear Problem

"I'll use the latest tech just because it's cool!"

Reality: If it doesn't solve a real problem, what's the point?

üí° The Sweet Spot: A project that's challenging enough to impress, realistic enough to finish, and solves an actual problem.

Case Study: MY Smart Bridge Router

How We Chose Our Winning Topic

The Problem We Identified üéØ

Public WiFi networks (hotels, cafes, airports) are insecure and expose users to:

  • Data interception and man-in-the-middle attacks
  • Privacy breaches and tracking
  • Limited control over content filtering
  • No bandwidth management

The need: A portable, secure, customizable router you can carry anywhere.

Why This Topic Worked ‚úÖ

‚úì Multiple Subfields:

  • Embedded Systems (Raspberry Pi)
  • Networking (OpenWrt, routing)
  • Web Development (control interface)
  • Security (VPN, firewall)
  • Voice Commands (IoT integration)

‚úì Realistic Scope:

  • Hardware: ~$100 Raspberry Pi + USB Wi-Fi Adapter
  • Software: Open source (free!)
  • Completable in one semester
  • Clear milestones

"My entire project relies on this free API..." ‚Üí API gets discontinued mid-semester.

‚ö†Ô∏è Requires Expensive Hardware You Don't Have

"I need a $2000 LiDAR sensor..." ‚Üí Budget says no.

Better: Work within your budget or secure funding early.

‚ö†Ô∏è No Way to Demo If Internet/Cloud Fails

"My app needs constant cloud connection..." ‚Üí WiFi dies during presentation.

Better: Always have offline demo capability.

‚ö†Ô∏è Requires Expertise in Something Brand New

"I'll learn quantum computing from scratch and build..." ‚Üí Not enough time.

Better: Build on skills you have, learn 1-2 new things max.

‚ö†Ô∏è Legal/Ethical Grey Areas

Where to Find Great Ideas

üí° Inspiration Sources

1. Your Own Pain Points

What frustrates you daily? What could be easier?

Example: Our router came from dealing with routers that offer no control.

2. Research Papers & IEEE Publications

Find cutting-edge ideas, then simplify for your scope.

üîó Resources: IEEE Xplore, Google Scholar, ArXiv

3. GitHub Trending & Open Source

See what developers are building. Can you improve it? Add features?

üîó Check: github.com/trending

4. Industry Problems (Internships/Jobs)

Saw inefficiencies at your internship? Build a solution!

5. Combine Existing Technologies in New Ways

Take proven concepts and merge them creatively.

Example: Raspberry Pi (existing) + OpenWrt (existing) + Web Interface (existing) = Our unique router

Pro Tip: Don't reinvent the wheel. Stand on the shoulders of giants and add your unique twist!

Brainstorming Your Topic

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: List Your Interests & Skills (5 min)

Write down everything you've learned in your CCE courses:

  • Programming languages you know
  • Hardware platforms (Arduino, Raspberry Pi, etc.)
  • Favorite courses/topics
  • Skills you WANT to learn

Step 2: Identify Problems (10 min)

What bugs you? What could be better?

  • In your daily life
  • On campus
  • In your family/community
  • In tech you use

Step 3: Match Problems with Skills (10 min)

Which problems can you solve with your skillset?

Step 4: Apply the 4-Question Framework (15 min)

Run your top 3-5 ideas through the questions from Slide 3.

Step 5: Talk to Your Advisor (Critical!)

Get feedback EARLY. They've seen hundreds of projects - trust their input.

Deliverable: By end of this module, you should have 2-3 solid topic ideas to discuss with your advisor!

Scoping: Must-Have vs. Nice-to-Have

Define Your MVP (Minimum Viable Project)

Our Router Example:

‚úÖ Must-Have (Core Features)

  • Basic routing functionality
  • VPN support
  • Web-based control panel
  • AdGuard content filtering
  • Basic firewall rules

These were non-negotiable for graduation.

üéÅ Nice-to-Have (Bonus)

  • Voice command integration
  • Advanced traffic analytics
  • Mobile app instead of web
  • Cloud sync features
  • Multi-language support

Added if time permits!

The 80/20 Rule

Aim to complete 80% of your project in the first 60% of the timeline. This gives you buffer for:

  • Unexpected bugs (there WILL be bugs)
  • Documentation time
  • Testing and refinement
  • Adding those nice-to-have features

Key Takeaways

Remember These üéØ

‚úÖ Choose a project that:

Solves a REAL problem (not just "cool tech")
Showcases MULTIPLE skills from your degree
Is REALISTIC to finish in one semester
Will look GREAT on your resume

üí° Pro Tips:

  • Start with problems YOU face - you'll be more motivated
  • Define MVP early - know what's essential vs. bonus
  • Get advisor feedback BEFORE you commit
  • Build on existing tech, don't reinvent everything
  • Always have a backup demo plan

üö© Avoid projects that:

  • Require expensive hardware you can't afford
  • Depend on unstable third-party services
  • Need you to learn too many new things at once
  • Have unclear legal/ethical implications
